# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Calibrate is for people who are trying to lose weight, become more fit, or build healthier eating habits without becoming nutrition analysts. They are often logging meals on mobile during a busy day, checking progress between routines, and looking for the next small adjustment that will help them keep momentum.

Users need the product to translate calories, macros, weight trends, and habit history into upbeat, practical guidance. They should be able to see what is working, understand what to do next, and drill into details only when those details will help them reach decisions more easily.

## Product Purpose

Calibrate exists to make nutrition tracking feel purposeful, selective, and humane. The app should help users log quickly, understand their daily and weekly trajectory, and discover high-impact adjustments without being buried in raw data.

Success means the interface imparts energy and life to the user, and while informing them around food and progress. The product should make insights feel actionable, overview-first, and easy to revisit as habits develop.

## Brand Personality

Vibrant, optimistic, and hyper-modern.

The product voice is energizing, motivating, and nourishing, fusing the organic freshness of a green apple with a restrained neon brightness. It can feel clinical in the positive sense: evidence-aware and precise, without becoming dull, gray, or lifeless. It should feel welcoming, with room for beautiful plant and leaf accents that make the experience feel healthier and more human.

Positive, fun motivation may become a future direction when it helps users feel supported. Coercive pressure, shame, streak anxiety, or manipulative habit mechanics are off-brand.

## Anti-references

Calibrate should not feel like a shame-heavy weight-loss app, generic startup SaaS demo, spreadsheet dashboard, sterile hospital portal, aggressive fitness tracker, coercive gamified habit app, medical-device interface, or vague AI insights product.

It should avoid moralizing food choices, red warning-heavy calorie language, before-and-after transformation pressure, and "crush your goals" fitness-bro energy. It should also avoid exposing every metric at once through dense table grids or tiny analytic panels. Charts are welcome when they quickly create useful overviews, trends, and choices.

Clean, professional, and organized is good. Gray, lifeless, intimidating, or mechanically clinical is not.

## Design Principles

1. Insight before data. Show the useful interpretation first, then let users drill deeper when detail improves the decision.
2. Momentum without shame. Encourage consistent action through supportive language, practical swaps, and visible progress without moralizing or pressure.
3. Calm task flow. Logging and review should feel quick, focused, and mobile-first, with progressive disclosure instead of dense dashboards.
4. Professional warmth. Preserve credibility and organization while adding enough organic, plant-informed humanity to avoid sterile health software.
5. Selective analytics. Every chart, metric, and trend must earn its place by helping the user choose a next step.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA for contrast, focus visibility, semantics, and keyboard access. Support reduced-motion preferences and avoid motion that delays task completion.

Macro charts and nutritional status indicators should be color-blind-safe and should not rely on color alone. Copy should stay low-anxiety, non-shaming, and clear for users who may feel vulnerable around food, weight, or habit change.

Mobile-first logging is a core accessibility concern: primary logging flows should remain readable, reachable, and efficient on small screens.
